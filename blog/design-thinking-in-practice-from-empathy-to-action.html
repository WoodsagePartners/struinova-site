Design Thinking in Practice: From Empathy to Action

By Ron Brumbarger · Indianapolis

The five phases are simple to list and hard to do. Almost everything that separates real design thinking from theater lives in the doing.

Nearly everyone has sat through a design thinking session. A facilitator teaches the phases, the room feels inspired, and a week later people are back in their silos working the old way. The framework was never the problem. The execution was. Real design thinking takes practice on live problems, honest facilitation, and the people who disagree sharing one room.

The five phases

01

Empathy. Genuine understanding of the people who live the problem. Grounded in interviews and observation, not personas invented at a whiteboard.

02

Define. The real problem emerges here, and it is rarely the one you started with. This is the reframe.

03

Ideate. Many possibilities, judgment deferred. Directed creativity, not a brainstorm for its own sake.

04

Prototype. Build something rough and real quickly. A sketch, a mockup, a demo. The point is to learn, not to impress.

05

Test. Put it in front of actual users, listen, iterate. Testing reveals the next problem, and the cycle continues.

Teams skip empathy because it is slow, over-build prototypes because polish feels safe, and test with the wrong people because it is convenient. Facilitation exists to stop all three.

Why facilitation is the whole game

The phases are easy to name and easy to shortcut. A good facilitator keeps the process honest: forcing real empathy before definition, keeping prototypes cheap enough to throw away, and making sure the people you test with are the people who actually live the problem. Without that discipline, design thinking becomes a vocabulary rather than a practice.

A field note

In Manitou Springs, Colorado we facilitated a room of roughly twenty-five people from organizations that had failed to work together before. Different priorities, real history, thin trust. Each group started with empathy for the others' actual constraints, which turned defining the problem into a shared act instead of a fight. Ideation happened in mixed teams. Prototypes stayed rough on purpose. They did not leave agreeing on everything. They left understanding each other, with a shared language and enough practice to keep collaborating after we were gone.

How it becomes a habit

Teams that work this way start asking better questions, prototyping before building, and testing before scaling. Best of all, they carry it back to their own groups, and it spreads. That is the aim. You are the hero here. We just hand you a clearer way to see and step back to watch you use it.
