The Resident Model: Why Embedded Innovation Works

By Ron Brumbarger · Indianapolis

The traditional consultant parachutes in, runs a session, leaves a deck, and disappears. The resident does almost the opposite, and the difference shows up months later.

There is a reason universities have used residencies for decades. You bring exceptional talent inside for a season, not as a visitor delivering a report but as a working member of the team. The idea travels to business intact. An Innovator in Residence is part fresh eyes, part facilitator, part trainer, and the whole point is that when the residency ends, the capability stays behind.

Two models, two very different endings

01

The parachute. Fly in, run a workshop, leave a polished deck, disappear. You are left with recommendations and no one who knows how to carry them.

02

The embed. Work alongside the team for a season, teach throughout, and step back once the method has taken root. You are left with people who own the capability.

You can't be your own dentist. But a good dentist also teaches you to brush.

Why being in the room changes the result

Fresh eyes plus real context. A resident does not yet know "how things work here," which is an asset, not a liability. The naive question shakes an old assumption loose. But they are close enough to the work to respect its actual realities.

Real-time problem-solving. When a new obstacle surfaces, and it will, the resident is present to adjust on the spot instead of hearing about it secondhand a month later.

Skill transfer over time. A two-day workshop cannot transfer deep capability. A season of practice, feedback, and iteration can, so the skill becomes the team's own.

Skin in the game. An embedded partner is judged on whether your people can keep solving problems after the engagement, not on the polish of a slide.

How a residency runs

Every engagement is shaped to fit, but a common shape is one to three days a week for eight to twelve weeks. Workshops, facilitated decisions, hands-on training, and the quiet work of building innovation habits that keep going after the resident leaves.

Thirty years of local proof

Ron built and sold Bitwise Solutions here in Indianapolis, grew and sold Apprentice University, and has served as an Entrepreneur in Residence. When the Indianapolis Metropolitan Police Department wanted a genuinely fresh approach to a decades-cold homicide, they came to Ron, and the team's method and playbook outlived the four-month engagement. That is the resident model in one sentence: the work ends, the capability does not.

When to choose it

Reach for a resident partner when the challenge is complex, when cultural change matters, and when you want your team to own the capability rather than rent it. Reach for traditional consulting when you need a one-time assessment or outside validation and nothing ongoing. Most organizations facing a real innovation problem are better served by the embed.
