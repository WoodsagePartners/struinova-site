Creative Problem-Solving Workshops

Your team is smart, hardworking, and stuck. That is almost never a people problem. It is a method problem, and method is teachable.

When a talented team stops moving, the instinct is to push harder on the same approach. Longer meetings. More brainstorms. Another offsite. The effort is real, and it mostly does not work, because the thing that went stale was not the effort. It was the way the problem was being seen.

We run workshops built on Simplexity, the eight-step creative problem-solving process from Basadur Innovation. Ron is a certified Simplexity facilitator and a Senior Research Fellow with Basadur. The method does not ask your team to "be more creative." It gives them a rhythm, diverge to open the possibility space, converge to narrow it, and defer judgment so no good idea dies before it is understood.

When you change how you see a problem, you change the problem you see.

The eight steps

01

Problem Finding. Before you solve, you find. The problem you were handed is rarely the one worth solving.

02

Fact Finding. Separate what you actually know from what everyone has simply been assuming.

03

Problem Definition. The hardest step and the most valuable. Name the real problem and everything downstream gets easier.

04

Idea Finding. Generate lots of possibilities, judgment deferred, nothing defended.

05

Evaluation and Selection. Test ideas against reality. Keep what holds.

06

Action Planning. Build the road from idea to execution, specific enough to actually move.

07

Gaining Acceptance. Bring the people who fund, approve, and carry it along for the reasoning, not just the result.

08

Taking Action. Move, measure, adjust. The loop keeps running.

What two days actually looks like

Day one is foundation. We start with connection before content, because ideas do not surface in a room where people feel unsafe. Then we teach the core skills and put them to work on your real challenge, not a case study. By the end of the day the team has usually redefined the problem it walked in with.

Day two is solve and scale. Teams generate, rank, and pressure-test ideas, then present real next steps with owners and timelines. You leave with a plan and, more importantly, with people who now know how the plan was made.

A field note

Manitou Springs, Colorado came to us in crisis. Homelessness and a mental-health emergency had spilled in from fast-growing Colorado Springs next door, tourism was sliding, and residents were blaming leadership. Roughly twenty-five people from rival organizations, several with self-serving agendas, sat down for two days. One walked out in the first hour. By lunch the next day, former adversaries were sharing tables and building a shared plan. The community conceived and stood up a wellness and referral hub, The Hive, that the Manitou Springs police began routing people to directly. And the participants carried the skills back into their own organizations and used them again. We solved the immediate problem. They kept the capability.

What your team keeps

This is the difference between a workshop and a training-circuit day. Consultants leave a deck. We leave a method, a shared vocabulary, and the muscle memory that comes from practicing on a problem that actually mattered. When the next stuck problem shows up, and it will, your people already know what to do with it.
